Stellara Labs — FY26 H1 Portfolio Review

Stellara Labs is a 180-person product company with four core divisions: Platform Engineering, Growth, Customer Success, and Applied Research. Over the first half of fiscal year 2026, the company shipped seven major initiatives across these divisions, each driven by a cross-functional team and sponsored by a member of the leadership group. What follows is a consolidated portfolio review for the executive committee, covering the projects, the people who led them, and the measurable impact delivered.

The most consequential launch of the half was Project Aurora, a real-time personalization engine built by the Growth division. Aurora was led by Maya Okafor, Director of Growth Engineering, with technical architecture owned by Daniyar Aitmatov, Principal Engineer, and data science led by Priya Venkatesan. The project replaced the legacy batch-recommendation pipeline, Helios, which had been operated by Customer Success since 2023. Aurora went to general availability in late February 2026 after a six-week staged rollout managed by Site Reliability lead Tomás Ferreira. The measured impact was substantial: average homepage click-through-rate increased 37 percent, session-to-purchase conversion improved 18 percent, and inference latency dropped from 850 milliseconds to 92 milliseconds at the 95th percentile. Aurora is now the foundation of three dependent initiatives scheduled for H2, including Project Nimbus, a push-notification targeting system being incubated by the Mobile team under Rohan Patel.

Within Platform Engineering, the headline project was Project Lattice, a migration from a monolithic order-management service to a domain-driven mesh of seventeen services. Lattice was sponsored by CTO Ingrid Halvorsen and technically led by Staff Engineer Eliot Reeves, with platform tooling built by the Developer Experience team under Angeline Ouédraogo. Lattice removed the single largest source of production incidents at Stellara: the legacy order-monolith had accounted for 62 percent of all P1 incidents in FY25, and since the mesh went live in January 2026, that share dropped to 11 percent. Deploy frequency across the order domain rose from weekly to multiple times per day, and the mean time to restore service after incidents fell from 44 minutes to 9 minutes. Eliot's team also introduced a shared developer platform, internally referred to as Runway, which is now used by every engineering team for CI, observability, and feature flags. Runway adoption stands at 97 percent of active services as of April 2026.

The Applied Research division, led by Chief Scientist Dr. Aoife Nakamura, delivered two significant outcomes. First, the Anomaly Detection Initiative, driven by research scientist Casper Lindqvist and engineering lead Ximena Barranco-Oliveira, replaced a third-party fraud-scoring vendor. The new in-house model reduced fraudulent transactions by 41 percent while decreasing false-positive user friction events by 28 percent; over the full half, it saved an estimated 4.2 million dollars in vendor fees and chargeback losses. Second, the Document Understanding Project, led by Nadia Al-Khouri with contributions from intern Henrik Osei, shipped an internal knowledge-extraction service that is now consumed by the Legal, Compliance, and Customer Success organizations. The service processes roughly 14,000 documents per week and has eliminated an estimated 9,600 hours of annual manual-review labor across those teams.

Customer Success, under VP Beatriz Moraes, focused H1 on a single operational project: Project Compass, a customer-health and churn-prediction platform that consumes outputs from both Aurora and the Anomaly Detection Initiative. Compass was built by Senior PM Linh Dao-Carter in partnership with data engineering lead Oluwaseun Adebayo and customer success manager Pierre Lacoste. Since launch in March 2026, Compass has flagged 312 at-risk accounts for proactive outreach, 78 percent of whom were successfully retained, compared with a historical save-rate of 34 percent through reactive ticket-driven workflows. Net revenue retention for the small-and-midsize-business segment, the cohort Compass primarily serves, climbed from 104 percent in H2 FY25 to 119 percent in H1 FY26, a segment-best for the company.

The Enablement and Talent team, though not a formal division, played a structural role in two cross-cutting initiatives. Learning-and-Development lead Suheil Rahman and Engineering Program Manager Grace Abelardo ran the Polaris Program, a twelve-week onboarding bootcamp that brought thirty-two new engineers onto the platform in Q1. Attrition among Polaris graduates stands at zero as of this report, versus a 14 percent six-month attrition rate for engineers onboarded through the previous ad-hoc process. Separately, Suheil and Grace chaired the Internal Mobility Task Force, which successfully transitioned eleven engineers between divisions without any attrition — Daniyar Aitmatov's move from Platform to Growth to lead Aurora was a product of this program.

Several projects are in the late-planning stage for H2 and warrant mention. Project Helios-Sunset, led by Tomás Ferreira, will fully decommission the legacy recommendation pipeline by July 2026. Project Beacon, sponsored by CEO Anika Chandrasekar and driven by VP of Product Marcus Wentworth, will unify Stellara's three product lines into a single purchasing experience; Beacon's architecture reviews are being run by Eliot Reeves and Ingrid Halvorsen. Project Kintsugi, still in research by Dr. Aoife Nakamura and Casper Lindqvist, will explore whether the Anomaly Detection model can be extended to detect model-drift in Aurora's personalization outputs, creating a feedback loop between the Research and Growth divisions.

Looking across the half, three themes emerge. First, consolidation of AI capabilities into owned infrastructure has reduced vendor spend by nearly six million dollars annualized while improving key quality metrics. Second, platform investments — Lattice and Runway — have materially improved reliability and engineering velocity, creating leverage for every team dependent on them. Third, the cross-divisional partnerships exemplified by Compass and the Internal Mobility Task Force suggest that Stellara's organizational boundaries have become unusually porous for a company its size. Anika Chandrasekar has asked Maya Okafor, Ingrid Halvorsen, Aoife Nakamura, and Beatriz Moraes to co-author the H2 strategic plan, with a commitment to preserve that porosity as the company scales toward an anticipated 280-person headcount by end of FY26.
